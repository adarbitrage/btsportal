Watch This First: What Is Affiliate Arbitrage?
A short overview of how this business model works — start here before anything else
💡 What is Affiliate Arbitrage? (Plain English)
Here's the basic idea: you promote someone else's product using paid ads. When someone clicks your ad, visits a landing page you've created, and then buys the product — you earn a commission. The "arbitrage" part means you're trying to spend less on ads than you make in commissions. Your goal is to find the right combination of ad, landing page, and audience that makes that happen consistently.

Key Terms — Plain English Definitions

Bookmark this section. Whenever you see an unfamiliar term in a video or in this guide, come back here first.

Affiliate Network
A marketplace where companies list products they want promoted. You sign up, pick a product, and get a unique tracking link. When someone buys through your link, you get paid. In The Blitz™: Media Mavens, ClickBank, Affiliati, and MaxWeb.
Commission / CPA
The money you earn each time someone buys the product you're promoting. CPA stands for "Cost Per Acquisition." Example: if you earn $50 per sale, your CPA is $50.
Landing Page
The web page someone sees after clicking your ad. Its job is to warm them up and get them interested enough to click through to the product's sales page. You create and control this page.
Advertorial
A type of landing page that reads like an article or editorial — it tells a story or shares information rather than being an obvious ad. Used in Media Mavens and some Affiliati campaigns. Some products include a pre-made advertorial you can use right away.
Jump Page / Bridge Page
A very short landing page — usually just a headline, an image, and a button — that sends visitors directly to a video sales letter (VSL). Used in ClickBank and MaxWeb campaigns.
VSL (Video Sales Letter)
A video that sells the product. The product owner creates it; your job is to get people to watch it. Many ClickBank and MaxWeb products use a VSL as their main selling tool.
Native Ad
An ad that blends into surrounding content — it looks like a recommended article rather than a traditional advertisement. On Caterpillar, you upload your headline (as plain text) and image (as a file) separately. The platform assembles the ad automatically.
Traffic Source / Publisher
The advertising platform that shows your ads to potential buyers. In this guide you're using Caterpillar — a native advertising platform. Grasshopper and Crane (banner ad platforms) are covered in separate Supplemental Guides.
Hero Shot
The main image on your landing page — the first big visual a visitor sees. It sets the emotional tone and needs to connect with your headline.
Split Testing (A/B Testing)
Showing different versions of your ads or landing pages to different visitors to see which performs better. Example: testing 10 different headlines to find the one that gets the most clicks.
CTR (Click-Through Rate)
The percentage of people who click your ad or landing page button. Ad CTR = people who clicked the ad. Landing Page CTR = people who clicked through to the product page.
ROAS (Return on Ad Spend)
How much you made back for every dollar spent on ads. Example: spend $1,000, earn $600 in commissions = 60% ROAS. Goal is eventually 100%+ (profitable).
DIYTrax
Your campaign tracking dashboard. It connects your ads, landing pages, and affiliate links, and records which combinations generate sales. Think of it as your campaign's control center.
Placement
The specific format and shape of your ad. On Caterpillar, placements are things like "16:9 static image," "9:16 animated GIF," or "16:9 video." You test different placements in Round 3 once you've identified a winning visual creative in Round 2.
Compliance
A review process where your ads and landing pages are checked against the affiliate network's and publisher's rules. You must submit your creative assets for compliance approval before going live.
Flexy™
The website builder used to create and host your landing pages. You clone a pre-built template and customize it with your own headlines, images, and copy — no coding required. For Media Mavens users, Flexy™ hosts the single page that holds the MetricMover™ embed code. For ClickBank users, Flexy™ hosts all 10 individual jump pages.
FreeAdCopy™
An AI copywriting tool provided by TCE for generating ad headlines and other marketing copy. Used to brainstorm and create ad headline variations for your Caterpillar campaigns.
AffAngleArchitect
A specialized AI bot on Poe.com, pre-configured to generate strong marketing angles for affiliate campaigns. Used to create advertorial headline angles (Media Mavens) and jump page angles (ClickBank/MaxWeb).
MetricMover™
A split-testing tool used for Media Mavens and Affiliati advertorial campaigns. It automatically rotates your 5 headlines × 5 hero shots (25 combinations) and tracks which combinations convert best.

Before You Start — What You Need to Know

Read this entire module before touching any technical setup. It covers what your money is actually buying, what realistic results look like, and how the three phases work together. Understanding this now prevents panic and confusion later.

What Your Budget Is Actually Buying

Your ad spend in The Blitz™ is buying data, not revenue. Think of it like a scientist running experiments — each one costs money and teaches you something. Early experiments almost always "fail" in the traditional sense, but they produce information that makes later experiments far more successful.

💡 What does "buying data" mean in practice?
When you spend $500 in Round 1, you're finding out which of your 10 ad headlines your audience responds to. That information is extremely valuable — without it, you'd be guessing in Round 2. The sales you make in Round 1 are a bonus. The real prize is knowing which headline wins so you can use it going forward.

Students who invest time in that training move faster.

💡 The Two-Headline Concept — Understanding the Difference
In affiliate marketing you'll be writing two different types of headlines, and they have two very different jobs:

Ad Headlines — These appear in your Caterpillar native ads. Their only job is to generate enough curiosity and interest to get someone to click. They don't need to explain everything — they just need to intrigue. Think of them like a movie trailer: enough to make you want to see more, but not the whole story.

Landing Page Headlines — These appear at the top of your landing page. Their job is to carry the story forward from the ad click, deepen the reader's engagement, and pull them into the advertorial or page content far enough that they're motivated to buy. They build on the curiosity the ad headline created.

Both are critical — but they work together, not independently. A great ad headline that leads to a mismatched landing page headline will kill conversions. The two need to feel like a connected, continuous experience for the reader.

Part 2 — Create Your Ad Headlines Everyone

Regardless of which affiliate network you're using, you'll need 10 ad headlines for your Caterpillar campaign. These are the short, punchy lines that appear in your native ads — designed purely to generate a click.

💬 What makes a great ad headline?
Great ad headlines are usually curiosity-driven, specific, or challenge a common assumption. They don't give everything away — they open a loop in the reader's mind that can only be closed by clicking. Examples of the kind of angles that work: "Doctors Can't Explain Why This Works So Well," "The One Thing Most People Get Wrong About Sleep," "This 60-Second Trick Is Changing How People Think About Energy." Notice how none of them sell the product outright — they create a question the reader wants answered.

✦ Media Mavens / Affiliati (with pre-built advertorial)
💡 How this path works
All Media Mavens products come with a pre-built advertorial — the body copy is already written for you. Your job is to find the most effective headline and hero shot to place at the top of that pre-built page. You'll create 5 headline variations and 5 hero shot image options. The MetricMover™ App then combines these into 25 unique landing pages — same body copy on every page, but a different headline and image at the top of each one. DIYTrax rotates visitors through all 25 and tracks which combinations generate the most sales.

Your target: 5 landing page headlines × 5 hero shot images = 25 combinations tested simultaneously.

💡 Why 25 combinations — and why does it take multiple rounds?
Instead of guessing which headline and image pair works best, you test all 25 at the same time. The MetricMover™ App creates 25 unique landing pages, and DIYTrax rotates visitors through them automatically — tracking which combinations generate the most sales.

Important: it will most likely take multiple rounds before a clear winner emerges. Here's why — each ad click goes to a different landing page in rotation. So 25 clicks on your ads results in only 1 click per landing page. That's not enough data to declare a winner. It generally takes multiple rounds to accumulate enough clicks across all 25 pages to see a definitive winner emerge — and that's completely normal. The data from each round builds on the last, weaker pages fall away, and your best-performing combinations rise to the top.

Generating Your 5 Hero Shot Images

Just like with headlines, your 5 hero shots should each represent a distinct angle or emotional approach — not just 5 variations of the same idea. Think of each image as its own hypothesis: one might convey hope and transformation, another might focus on pain and frustration, a third might lean into credibility or scientific authority. Each angle attracts a different type of reader, and you won't know which resonates most until you test them.

A strong hero shot does two things simultaneously: it reinforces the specific headline it's paired with, and it creates an emotional connection that makes the reader want to keep scrolling. A mismatch between headline and image — even a subtle one — can kill engagement before the reader gets to the first sentence of the advertorial. Think of each headline and hero shot as a pair, not two independent elements.

Step 4 — Create Your Caterpillar Ad Creative Everyone

💡 How Caterpillar native ads work
Caterpillar is a native advertising platform. Your ads look like recommended articles rather than obvious advertisements. You upload three things separately: your headlines (as plain text), one description (as plain text), and one image file. The platform assembles the ad automatically. You do NOT design a complete banner image.

Round 1 Targets

  • 10 Headlines — each 90 characters or fewer (roughly the length of a tweet)
  • 1 Description — 90 characters or fewer, appears beneath the headline in the ad
  • 1 Image — 16:9 ratio (landscape), minimum 960×540px, recommended 1280×720px or larger

Know your targets before opening any creative tool. Build with these specifications in mind from the start.

💬 What makes a great Caterpillar ad image?
Your ad image has one job: stop the scroll. A person browsing a news site sees dozens of content recommendations — your image needs to be the one that makes them pause. Here's what tends to work:
  • Emotional resonance — images that trigger curiosity, surprise, concern, or aspiration outperform "neutral" imagery. Ask yourself: does this image make me feel something?
  • Human faces — particularly faces showing emotion, draw the eye naturally and often outperform product or abstract shots
  • Visual contrast — bold colors or strong light-vs-dark contrast stand out against the typical muted tones of news content around your ad
  • Relevance to the headline — the image and headline should feel like they belong together. A reader who clicks should feel the ad delivered on what the image and headline promised
  • Avoid generic stock photo aesthetics — overly polished, obviously staged images tend to underperform. More authentic or unexpected visuals typically get better click-through rates
Remember: in Round 1 you're testing with one image. You don't need it to be perfect — you need it to be a strong starting point. Round 2 is specifically for testing different visual approaches against each other.

Module 3

Scale — Multiply Your Profits

Scaling is about spending more on what's already proven to work. You are not introducing new ideas at this stage. Save experimentation for new campaigns — scaling is for proven winners only.
Prerequisites — All Must Be Met Before ScalingRounds 1, 2, and 3 complete. Winning visual creative identified (Round 2). Winning placement format identified (Round 3). Round 3 success criteria met (60%+ ROAS).
Method 1

Increase Budget on Winners

Increase daily budget and CPC bids on profitable placements. Remove all non-profitable ads first. Scale 2×, then 5×, then 10× as performance holds. If profiting $50/day at $100 spend → test $200/day, then $500/day.

Method 2

Test New Placements

Take your proven ads and landing pages to new placements. Budget $1,500 minimum per new placement on Grasshopper or Crane. Use only proven creative — no new experiments here.

Method 3

Graduate to Master Publisher

Dedicated email blast to a large subscriber list. Much larger reach, higher risk. Only after 14+ consecutive days of profitability on current publishers.

How to Read Your Data While Scaling

Scaling isn't just increasing budget — it's a monitored process. As you increase spend, watch for these signals:

What You SeeWhat It MeansWhat to Do
ROAS stays stable as budget increasesCampaign is scaling cleanly — the audience is large enough to absorb more spendContinue scaling. Try 5× then 10× budget.
ROAS drops as budget increasesYou're starting to saturate the available audience — fewer high-quality matches leftHold current budget. Look to expand to a new placement or publisher instead.
ROAS drops suddenly without a budget changeAd fatigue — the same audience has seen your ad too many timesRefresh your ad creative or introduce a new headline variation.
Performance varies significantly day to dayNormal fluctuation — don't react to single daysEvaluate on weekly averages, not daily numbers.

When to Stop Scaling a Placement

Know when a placement has given you everything it can. Stop aggressively scaling when:

  • ROAS has declined for 5+ consecutive days despite no changes to budget or creative
  • You've increased budget 3× or more and performance hasn't improved proportionally
  • Click volume is dropping while budget remains constant (audience saturation)

This doesn't mean the campaign is dead — it means it's time to expand to a new placement or publisher rather than pushing more into the current one.
