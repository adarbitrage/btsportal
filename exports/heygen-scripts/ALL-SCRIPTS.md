## 034. Understanding Copy Blocks for Effective Headlines

*Original title: Copy Blocks Training 2 · Video ID: x2mY7I97jV5eakzw*

In this video, I'll show you a concept called copy blocks. Over the next 20 minutes, we'll explore how this idea can enhance your copywriting. Many of you may have encountered this in my Cyborg call, and I found it incredibly helpful. I've been using it consistently, and I think you'll find it beneficial too.

Copy blocks, a term coined by copywriter Luke Iha, likens writing to assembling with Lego blocks. This model is particularly useful for crafting headlines and analyzing longer pieces of copy. While it isn't a complete system, it offers a fun and effective way to approach your writing. For example, let's look at a strong headline: "Nine figure copywriter reveals new wildcard copywriter method that allows struggling freelancers to land high paying clients and make 10K plus a month without cold outreach, Upwork, or job boards." 

What makes this headline effective? Elements like "nine figure" and "10K a month" grab attention and appeal to aspirations. The phrase "struggling freelancers" highlights pain points, while "landing high paying clients" serves as a clear goal. We can categorize these elements into blocks: the "nine figure copywriter" acts as a proof block, establishing authority; "new wildcard copywriter method" evokes curiosity; "struggling freelancers" represents pain; and "without cold outreach, Upwork, or job boards" addresses constraints.

As we analyze headlines, we can identify these blocks: proof, curiosity, pain, promise, and constraint. Each serves a purpose and can strengthen your copy. For instance, if a headline lacks a certain block, consider how you might add it to enhance the overall impact. 

To illustrate, think of your customer avatar standing on one side of a canyon, representing their current pain. The promise block is the other side, their desired outcome, while the curiosity block is the bridge that connects the two. The proof block builds trust, and constraints are the mental baggage that must be addressed for them to take that first step.

Now, let's apply this to another headline: "Portugal's most notorious playboy reveals how to go from struggling with women to effortlessly dating high quality nines and tens using the automatic attraction system. ## 052. Choosing Between MetricMover and Individual Landing Pages

*Original title: How to Know Whether to Use MetricMover or Individual Landing Pages · Video ID: kH8f1WPQ5KaC60Kl*

In this video, I'll show you how to determine whether to set up a metric mover campaign or use cloned Flexy pages when split testing your landing pages. At this stage, you've gathered your marketing assets, whether that's an advertorial for your landing page or a jump bridge page for your product. You have variants to set up, and you're wondering how to decide between a metric mover project or cloned Flexy pages.

For a metric mover, the main content remains constant while you test a few select variables, such as a headline and a hero shot. Typically, you'll want to test one to three variables at most. This is important because the number of combinations can grow quickly. For instance, if you have five headlines and five hero shots, that results in 25 total page variants. If you add five different sub-headlines, you’re looking at 125 page variants. To achieve statistical significance, you'll need a substantial number of clicks on those pages. Therefore, it's best to keep your sets smaller or have a larger set of one variable paired with a smaller set of another.

On the other hand, you should clone Flexy pages when you have headlines and hero shots that are intentionally paired together. For example, headline number two is crafted to go with hero shot number two, and you don’t want them to mix with other combinations. This approach is also suitable when the page content differs significantly, such as testing different angles, lengths, or body content. If you're changing multiple elements on a page, like different images or texts, you’ll want to create separate Flexy pages.

To sum up, use metric mover when you want to mix and match elements while keeping the main content constant. Use cloned Flexy pages when you want to intentionally pair variables or when the content varies significantly across pages. Based on your assets and what you are testing, you can now choose between a metric mover campaign or cloned Flexy pages. We'll dive deeper into this in the next videos.
